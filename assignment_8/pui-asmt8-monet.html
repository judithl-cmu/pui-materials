Claude Oscar Monet (1840-1926) was a French painter and a founder of Impressionist painting. He was a prolific and consistent practitioner of the movement’s philosophy - to position oneself in front of nature, especially as applied to plein air landscape painting.
The concept of Impressionism comes from one of Monet’s most famous paintings, Impression: Sunrise. By striving to translate his unique perception of the natural world directly to the canvas, the artist was instrumental in forging an entirely new direction for the world of art.
Reacting against Romanticism, Realistic artists such as Gustave Courbet focused on accuracy in depicting their subject matter, which often consisted of working-class figures and unembellished, natural scenery.

Early Years / 1860s

1862
During this period from his late teens to early 20s, Monet spent time in the Louvre in Paris, where he met contemporary painters, such as Edouard Manet, who would later become colleagues.
Disillusioned with the traditional art taught at art schools, Monet became a student of Charles Gleyre in Paris, where he met Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Frédéric Bazille and Alfred Sisley. Together they shared new approaches to art, painting the effects of light en plein air with broken colour and rapid brushstrokes, in what later came to be known as Impressionism.

Late 1860s
Monet and other like-minded artists (including Paul Cezanne, Camille Pissarro, Alfred Sisley and Edgar Degas) met with rejection from the conservative Académie des Beaux-Arts, which held its annual exhibition at the Salon de Paris.

Impressionism / 1870s

1872
Impression: Sunrise was painted in 1872. From the painting, art critic Louis Leroy coined the term “Impressionism”.
In 1873, the artists formed their own arts organization, "Societe Anonyme Coopérative des Artistes Peintres, Sculpteurs et Graveurs," or the Cooperative and Anonymous Association of Painters, Sculptors and Engravers.

1874
The first Impressionist exhibition was held in 1874 and the main purpose was not so much for the participants to promote a new art style as to free themselves from the constraints of Salon de Paris.

Vétheuil and Giverny / 1880s

In September 1879, Camille, wife of Monet, died at the age of 32. After several difficult months following the death of Camille, Monet created some of his best paintings in the 19th century.
During the early 1880s, Monet painted several groups of landscapes and seascapes in what he considered to be campaigns to document the French countryside. In 1883, Monet bought a house in Giverny, where he planted a large garden and where he painted.

Series / 1890s

Monet's ambition of documenting the French countryside led him to adopt a method of painting the same scene many times in order to capture the changing of light and the passing of the seasons.

One of Monet’s most famous series is that of Rouen Cathedral, created in the 1892 and 1893 in Rouen, Normandy, then reworked in Monet’s studio in 1894.

Painting light gave Monet intense difficulties because of its extreme subtlety and ever-changing nature. However, his ability to capture the essence of the scenes quickly, which helped him achieve his desired effects.

The cathedral allowed him to highlight the paradox between a seemingly permanent, solid structure and the ever-changing light which constantly plays with our perception of it.

Late Years / 1890s Onwards

1893
Monet purchased additional land with a water meadow. In 1893 he began a vast landscaping project which included lily ponds that would become the subjects of his best-known works.

1899
Monet began painting the waterlilies, first in vertical views with a Japanese bridge as a central feature, and later in the series of large-scale paintings that was to occupy him continuously for the next 20 years of his life. The artist had painted his first renditions in the 1890s but produced the majority of the works during the final two decades of his life.
After losing both his second wife, Alice, and his eldest son, Jean, in 1911 and 1914 respectively, the artist sought consolation by embarking upon a great new work, a series of massive Water Lilies panels.
